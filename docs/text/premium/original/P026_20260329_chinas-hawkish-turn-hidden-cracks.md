# The Hardening of Chinese Public Opinion — And Its Hidden Cracks

> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 29, 2026
> Note: Original premium lesson synthesized from reference materials and supplementary research

A dangerous feedback loop is forming between Chinese public sentiment and state policy on Taiwan. Recent survey data from the Carter Center, drawn from interviews with roughly 6,500 Chinese citizens conducted between 2024 and 2025, reveals a sharp acceleration in hawkish attitudes that may be constraining Beijing's diplomatic flexibility. Yet buried within the same data are surprising pockets of pragmatism that China's official rhetoric works hard to conceal.

The numbers paint a striking picture of a public growing more combative. Support for using military force to achieve unification with Taiwan has nearly doubled, climbing from around 25 percent in early 2024 to close to half of respondents by late 2025. Over the same period, opposition to a military option fell from above 50 percent to just 38 percent. Attitudes toward the United States have hardened in parallel: nearly three-quarters of those surveyed now identify America as a threat to national security, and sentiment toward Washington scores a dismal 34 to 37 on a hundred-point scale. Perhaps most tellingly, 62 percent of respondents endorsed retaliatory measures against American trade policies — including restricting rare-earth exports — even when told such actions would inflict economic pain on China itself. A mere four percent favored negotiating over semiconductor export controls.

What makes these trends particularly consequential is the mechanism behind them. Public opinion in China is, as analysts have noted, elastic and highly responsive to state-driven narratives. When official media frames every American tariff as an act of aggression, citizens internalize the siege mentality, which in turn validates further escalation by policymakers who can claim popular mandate. The relationship between wealth and hawkishness adds a counterintuitive dimension: higher-income Chinese tend to be more culturally favorable toward America yet simultaneously more assertive about projecting Chinese power abroad. This suggests that exposure to the outside world does not automatically moderate nationalist impulses — it can sharpen them.

Yet the survey findings also expose genuine daylight between the public and the party line. Some 44 percent of respondents oppose the idea of sending Chinese troops to support Russia in its war against Ukraine, a position at odds with Beijing's tacit alignment with Moscow. Nearly half indicated willingness to compromise on certain South China Sea territorial claims if the United States reduced its regional military presence. These are not trivial numbers. They suggest a population capable of strategic calculation, one that weighs costs and benefits rather than reflexively following nationalist scripts.

The policy implications are sobering but not hopeless. If Washington recognizes that Chinese public opinion contains both hardening resolve and latent pragmatism, it can craft approaches that engage the latter without triggering the former. Dismiss these nuances, however, and the feedback loop tightens — making compromise progressively harder for leaders on both sides of the Pacific.
