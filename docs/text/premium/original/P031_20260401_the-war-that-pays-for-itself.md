# The War That Pays for Itself

Eastern Congo has endured more peace agreements than most conflicts produce battles. The latest, brokered by Washington and signed in December 2025 by the presidents of Congo and Rwanda, was supposed to silence the guns — yet three months later, fighting has spread deeper into South Kivu province, displacing half a million civilians and pushing toward Katanga, the industrial heartland that holds nine of the world's ten largest cobalt mines. The pattern is not new, and the reason is structural: this is a war that generates more wealth for its participants than peace would deliver.

The economics are stark. The Democratic Republic of Congo sits atop an estimated $24 trillion in mineral reserves and produces roughly 80 percent of the world's cobalt, a metal essential for every electric vehicle battery and smartphone on the planet. M23, the Rwandan-backed militia driving the current escalation, earns approximately $300,000 per month from its control of the Rubaya coltan mine alone, with at least 150 tons of tantalum ore smuggled across the border annually. For an armed group sustained by an estimated 4,000 Rwandan soldiers, this revenue transforms violence from a political instrument into a self-financing enterprise.

Diplomatic interventions have failed to alter this calculus. M23's January withdrawal from Uvira, presented as a goodwill gesture, was a tactical repositioning under American pressure; within weeks, fighting intensified in the surrounding highlands. Washington imposed sanctions on Rwanda's defence forces in March, yet the measures targeted individuals rather than the mineral supply chains that finance the conflict — the equivalent of fining a company's executives while leaving its most profitable division untouched.

The humanitarian consequences are devastating: more than seven million Congolese are now internally displaced, the largest such crisis on earth, and 25 million face acute food insecurity. Health systems across eastern provinces have buckled under outbreaks of cholera, measles, and mpox in camps built for thousands but sheltering tens of thousands. Until the financial architecture sustaining this war is dismantled — through enforceable mineral traceability and genuine penalties on nations profiting from smuggled resources — ceasefire agreements will continue to function as intermissions rather than conclusions. The conflict endures not despite diplomatic efforts, but because no agreement has yet made peace more profitable than war.
