# The Last Ten Percent

Even as solar panels and wind turbines claim ever-larger shares of the world's electricity mix, a stubborn arithmetic haunts the energy transition: the final fraction of power that renewables cannot reliably supply gives natural gas an outsized hold on energy security. A grid that draws ninety percent of its annual electricity from clean sources does not run on a steady nine-to-one blend. Instead, it operates almost entirely on renewables most of the time — and then, for roughly a tenth of all hours, depends almost entirely on gas. Those hours, when the wind dies and clouds linger and batteries drain, are the moments that define whether a country keeps its lights on.

This structural dependency explains why the current disruption to Gulf shipping lanes has sent power prices surging across continents even in countries that have invested heavily in renewables. Spain, which has built one of Europe's most aggressive solar and wind portfolios, still relies on gas to set power prices fifteen percent of the time — a figure that rises sharply during winter evenings and prolonged calm weather. The International Energy Agency noted strong gas-price volatility across Asia, Europe, and North America in January 2026, driven by adverse weather and geopolitical tensions. Countries that assumed their growing renewable capacity would insulate them from fossil-fuel shocks are discovering that dependency does not decline in proportion to average consumption.

The technologies that could eventually fill this gap remain years from commercial scale. Iron-air batteries, which promise storage lasting days rather than hours at a fraction of lithium-ion costs, have begun their first utility-scale deployments in the United States, with one manufacturer targeting an installed cost below twenty dollars per kilowatt-hour. Hydrogen storage and advanced flow batteries offer theoretical solutions for seasonal imbalances, but the global market for long-duration storage reached just $3.6 billion in 2025 — a fraction of what would be required to displace gas as the grid's backstop. New nuclear plants remain prohibitively expensive, and hydroelectric capacity depends on geography and rainfall that climate change is making less predictable.

The paradox deepens as gas markets themselves evolve. The IEA projects a sixty-five-billion-cubic-meter surplus of liquefied natural gas by 2030 as new export terminals flood the market, yet much of that supply is concentrated in North America, which accounted for over eighty-five percent of global LNG supply growth in 2026. Several emerging economies in Asia are bypassing gas infrastructure altogether, moving directly from coal to solar. If global gas demand falls while supply concentrates among fewer producers, the market could become thinner and more prone to the kind of acute shortages that the current crisis exemplifies. Gas may power a smaller share of the world's electricity in the future, but the hours it covers will remain the most consequential — and the most expensive.
