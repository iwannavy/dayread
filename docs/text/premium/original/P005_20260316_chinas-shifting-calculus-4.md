# The Hawk and the Tourist

A series of opinion polls conducted in 2024 and 2025, surveying sixty-five hundred mainland Chinese citizens, found that support for a military solution to the Taiwan question had risen from roughly a quarter to nearly half. Read in isolation, the numbers suggest a public hardening toward confrontation and a government that would face little domestic resistance to escalation. But a closer look at the same population reveals a more complicated picture — one that may constrain Beijing as much as it empowers it.

A 2025 survey by the Chicago Council on Global Affairs and the Carter Center, conducted through random-digit dialing across mainland China, found that while the public broadly favors progress toward unification, it consistently prefers negotiation over the use of force. A separate Brookings Institution analysis argued that Chinese citizens' genuine affection for Taiwanese people — their culture, their media, their food — may itself function as a brake on conflict. The paradox is sharpest among high-income respondents, who tend to consume more Taiwanese and American cultural products yet simultaneously express stronger support for projecting Chinese military power. Nationalism and cultural admiration, it turns out, are not opposites but cohabitants.

On the other side of the strait, the picture is equally layered. A November 2025 poll found that ninety percent of Taiwanese view China unfavorably, yet a separate defense survey showed that the share perceiving China's territorial ambitions as a serious threat actually declined from 2024 to 2025. Among supporters of the ruling Democratic Progressive Party, seventy-seven percent see the threat as serious; among opposition voters, the figure drops to around forty percent. Taiwanese society is less unified in its alarm than international headlines suggest, and Beijing's strategists must account for an adversary whose resolve is partisan rather than monolithic.

What these cross-strait surveys reveal is that public opinion on both sides is too fractured to serve as a reliable mandate for action. Chinese hawks exist alongside Chinese admirers of Taipei's night markets; Taiwanese anxiety coexists with Taiwanese complacency. For a government that has staked its legitimacy on eventual unification, the polling data offers encouragement and caution in equal measure — the appetite for assertiveness is real, but so is the public's preference that someone else bear the cost.
