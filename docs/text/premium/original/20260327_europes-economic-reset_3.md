# Europe's Economic Reset: From Brexit to Rearmament
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 27, 2026
> Note: Original premium lesson synthesized from internal reference pack

Two of the continent's largest economies are rethinking fundamental assumptions about growth, trade, and security, and the Gulf conflict is accelerating decisions that might otherwise have taken years. Britain is reversing course on its post-Brexit isolation, while Germany is attempting to spend its way out of stagnation through the largest rearmament program in its postwar history.

Britain's government has announced a decisive tilt toward the European Union, acknowledging that the economic cost of separation may have reached as high as eight percent of GDP. The chancellor has called for broad alignment with EU market rules, a reversal of the previous decade's push for regulatory independence. Polls now show the public would vote to rejoin by a wide margin, and the political calculation has shifted: Europe has moved from being a divisive issue to one that offers electoral advantage. Yet the government still rules out rejoining the single market or customs union outright and opposes restoring free movement, constraints that may limit how far negotiations can go.

Germany, meanwhile, is channeling enormous resources into defence. After decades of minimal military spending, the government has more than doubled its defence budget to roughly one hundred and eight billion euros, approaching three percent of GDP, with a target of three-and-a-half percent by 2029. A five-hundred-billion-euro infrastructure fund has been created alongside these military commitments. Manufacturing orders have begun to grow, likely driven by weapons and ammunition procurement, but converting orders into production remains slow. The broader economy grew just 0.2 percent last year, manufacturing output has fallen fifteen percent from its 2018 peak, and the country continues to lose about fourteen thousand manufacturing jobs each month.

The Gulf war is amplifying these pressures across the continent. In Britain, petrol prices jumped within days of hostilities beginning, though high fuel taxes mean pump prices remain less volatile than elsewhere. Despite the spike, the real cost of driving has rarely been lower thanks to frozen fuel duties and dramatic improvements in vehicle efficiency over the past three decades. Germany's energy-intensive industries, still adjusting to the loss of cheap Russian gas, face a fresh round of disruption as global supply routes constrict further.

Both countries illustrate a broader European pattern: old economic models are failing, and the search for new ones is being conducted under crisis conditions rather than at a pace of deliberate reform.
