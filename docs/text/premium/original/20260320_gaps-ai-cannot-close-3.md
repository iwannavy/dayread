# The Gaps AI Still Cannot Close
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 20, 2026
> Note: Original premium lesson synthesized from internal reference pack

Artificial intelligence has transformed industries from finance to health care, but several stubborn gaps are proving difficult to close. One of the most consequential affects the hundreds of millions of people who do not speak English. Research published in late 2025 found that the accuracy of leading language models in non-English languages was between twelve and twenty-nine percentage points lower than in English, with some models scoring as low as twenty-three percent on queries posed in African languages. Because models break text into small units called tokens, and that process is optimized for English, the same question can cost up to five times more to process in a language like Yoruba.

The language gap carries real-world stakes. A fifty-million-dollar initiative to deploy AI tools in a thousand primary health clinics across Africa risks offering inferior medical advice simply because patients speak local languages. Even explicitly multilingual models often retrieve facts internally in English and translate only at the final step, introducing additional errors at every stage. Fine-tuning with small amounts of high-quality non-English data can improve accuracy by more than five percentage points, but the dominant economic incentives still favor English-first development.

A parallel limitation is emerging in hardware. The shift from training AI models to running them in real time, a process known as inference, is exposing the weaknesses of current processors. Processor speeds have kept pace with demand, roughly tripling over successive hardware generations, but the channels that ferry data between a chip and its external memory have lagged far behind, gaining only about sixty percent per cycle. Engineers refer to this widening mismatch as a memory wall. Several startups are now designing chips that tackle this bottleneck through larger on-chip memory, in-memory computing, or architectures tailored specifically to transformer models, though the pace of algorithmic change means today's specialized hardware could quickly become obsolete.

Meanwhile, in India's outsourcing sector, which generated more than three hundred and fifteen billion dollars in combined revenue this year, the feared mass automation of coding has not materialized. Deploying AI tools in legacy software environments with poor documentation and intertwined systems remains far harder than building new applications from scratch. Outsourcing firms are repositioning as strategic AI consultants rather than code factories, and their hiring has continued despite widespread predictions of displacement.

The common thread across these domains is that artificial intelligence excels in controlled, English-language, greenfield environments but struggles with the messy complexity of the real world. Closing these gaps will require not just better models but more diverse training data, more efficient hardware, and sustained investment in the populations and systems that current technology serves least well.
