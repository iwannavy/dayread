# The Cost That Doubled

When Britain voted to leave the European Union in 2016, the Office for Budget Responsibility projected that Brexit would reduce long-run productivity by roughly four percent — painful but manageable, the kind of drag a growing economy could absorb over time. A decade later, the damage appears to have exceeded even cautious forecasts. An NBER study led by Bloom estimated the actual GDP reduction at six to eight percent, with firm-level investment running about twelve percent below counterfactual levels and employment three to four percent lower. The gap between early projections and observed outcomes is not a rounding error; it is the difference between a calculated risk and a miscalculation.

That gap explains why Rachel Reeves, Britain's chancellor, used her annual Mais lecture in March to declare that regulatory alignment with the EU should be the norm rather than the exception. The statement was less a philosophical conversion than an arithmetic one. Britain's post-Brexit trade deals with non-European partners have collectively generated less growth than a single agricultural agreement with the EU would deliver. Rather than the sudden shock that economists initially feared, the damage has been cumulative and diffuse — higher paperwork costs, lost trading relationships, businesses quietly giving up on European markets altogether.

The political arithmetic has shifted in parallel. Polls now show fifty-two percent of Britons would vote to rejoin, and Labour is losing progressive voters to the Greens and Liberal Democrats in part because its earlier caution on Europe no longer serves any electoral purpose. What was once a third rail in British politics — admitting that Brexit had been a mistake — has become something closer to a political requirement. Reeves acknowledged as much when she said the country risked being stranded between powerful trading blocs if it failed to choose a side.

Yet acknowledgment is not the same as reversal. Labour still refuses to rejoin the single market, restore free movement, or hold a new referendum. EU negotiators, who watched a similar alignment pitch from Theresa May collapse into Boris Johnson's hard exit, remain skeptical. Their position, as one analyst characterized it, is unchanged: welcome the sentiment, but show us the specifics. Britain has conceded the principle that gravity pulls toward Europe; whether it is willing to pay the full fare remains an open question.
