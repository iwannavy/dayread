# American Power: Symbols, Cyberspace, and the Exodus

The decision to place a sitting president's image on a commemorative gold coin for the first time in a century marks a break with longstanding democratic norms about the symbolism of currency. Under a longstanding legal prohibition, no sitting president's likeness may appear on standard American coins or notes. The administration sidestepped this restriction through a provision that allows the mint to produce commemorative pieces without a congressional vote. The last time a living American leader appeared on a coin was in 1926, during celebrations of the nation's hundred and fiftieth anniversary. The new coin, approved by a presidential commission, shows the president in a deliberately imposing pose with the word "liberty" inscribed above. The move fits a broader pattern of self-promotion across government buildings and official documents, raising questions about where political branding ends and personality cult begins.

On the security front, a new national cyber-strategy published this month signals a decisive shift from defense to offense. The seven-page document calls for pre-emptive disruption of threats before they breach domestic networks and, more controversially, encourages private firms to conduct retaliatory operations against attackers, a practice currently prohibited and often described as cyber-vigilante justice. The strategy arrives as state-backed hacking groups linked to the Gulf conflict have intensified attacks on American companies, with one recent breach reportedly destroying data across more than two hundred thousand corporate servers. Yet the agencies tasked with executing this aggressive posture are themselves in disarray: the military cyber command went without confirmed leadership for nearly a year, and the civilian cybersecurity agency has been weakened by political disputes over election security. Critics argue that offensive capability and defensive resilience are fundamentally different disciplines, and that success in disrupting foreign networks does little to protect domestic infrastructure from sabotage.

Meanwhile, a quieter transformation is unfolding as record numbers of Western citizens, including a growing share of Americans, leave their home countries. Comprehensive data from thirty-one nations show roughly four million people emigrating in the most recent year measured, about twenty percent more than before the pandemic. Taxes, remote work, and political disenchantment are driving the trend, with surveys showing declining faith in democratic institutions across the political spectrum. The emergence of a genuine expat economy, with multinational firms now employing over a third more workers abroad than before the pandemic, suggests the trend may be structural rather than cyclical. The consequences for sending countries include lost tax revenue, strained public services, and a potential deepening of political polarization, as research suggests that emigrants tend to hold more liberal views than those who remain.

These three stories share an underlying tension: a country projecting strength through grand gestures and aggressive strategies while quietly losing some of the citizens and institutional stability on which lasting power depends.
