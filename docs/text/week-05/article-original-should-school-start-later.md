# Should School Start Later?
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 2026
> Note: Week 5 — Opinions in Daily Life (difficulty 3)

Many parents and teachers have been asking the same question for years: should school start later in the morning?

Most middle and high schools begin classes around eight o'clock, which means students often wake up before seven.

Research on teenage sleep patterns shows that young people need at least eight to nine hours of sleep each night, but most teenagers report getting far less than that.

One major study found that students who slept enough performed better on tests, paid more attention in class, and reported feeling happier overall.

Because their bodies naturally want to fall asleep later and wake up later, forcing teenagers into early schedules works against their biology.

Several school districts in the United States have already pushed start times to eight-thirty or even nine o'clock, and the early results are encouraging.

Teachers in those districts say that students seem more awake during first period and that behavior problems have decreased since the change.

However, shifting the school schedule creates real challenges for families and communities.

Parents who work early shifts may struggle to find childcare if school begins later, and after-school sports and clubs would have to end later in the evening.

Bus schedules, which often serve elementary and secondary schools on the same routes, would also need a complete redesign.

Some critics argue that teenagers should simply learn to go to bed earlier, rather than expecting the entire system to adjust around them.

That is a fair point, but decades of sleep science suggest that biology is not something willpower alone can overcome.

Schools should at least consider a modest delay — even thirty minutes could make a meaningful difference.

The goal is not to make life easier for students who stay up too late playing games, but to respect what science tells us about how young brains develop and recover.

When a policy change is backed by solid evidence and the benefits clearly outweigh the costs, we should be willing to try it.
