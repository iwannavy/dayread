# Do We Need More Quiet Spaces?
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 2026
> Note: Week 5 — Opinions in Daily Life (difficulty 3)

Have you ever tried to read a book or finish an important email in a noisy café and found it almost impossible to concentrate?

Noise is everywhere in modern life — from traffic and construction outside to music and conversations inside — and many people have simply accepted it as normal.

But should we accept it, or should cities and buildings do more to create places where people can think without constant interruption?

Research has shown that prolonged exposure to background noise raises stress levels, reduces the ability to focus, and can even affect long-term health.

Office workers in open-plan layouts frequently report that they lose productive time because they cannot block out the sounds around them.

Students who study in noisy environments tend to score lower on memory tests than those who study in quieter settings.

These findings suggest that quiet is not a luxury — it is a basic condition that our brains need in order to function well.

However, not everyone agrees that public spaces must be quiet.

Some people argue that a certain level of background noise actually helps them feel comfortable and connected to the world around them.

Cafés, parks, and shared workspaces are social environments by nature, and enforcing silence in those places could make them feel cold and unwelcoming.

There is also the practical challenge: creating and maintaining truly quiet zones in a busy city requires careful design and ongoing investment.

Still, the solution does not have to be all or nothing.

Cities should consider building more libraries, reading rooms, and designated quiet areas in parks where silence is the expected norm.

Workplaces must offer private rooms or quiet pods alongside open areas so that employees can choose the environment that suits their current task.

The question is not whether noise should disappear entirely, but whether we are giving people enough options to find silence when they need it.
