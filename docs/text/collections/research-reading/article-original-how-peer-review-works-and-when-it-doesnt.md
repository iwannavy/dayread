# How Peer Review Works — And When It Doesn't
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 2026
> Note: Collection — Research Reading (difficulty 6)

The peer review system — the process by which scientific manuscripts are evaluated by independent experts before publication — is often described as the cornerstone of scientific quality control, the mechanism that separates rigorous research from speculation and ensures that the knowledge base upon which society depends is sound, reliable, and trustworthy. This description is not wrong, exactly, but it is dangerously incomplete, because it implies a level of reliability and consistency that the system has never, in practice, been able to deliver. The reality, as a growing body of evidence makes clear, is that peer review is a deeply imperfect process — one that catches many errors but misses many others, that is vulnerable to bias, manipulation, and outright fraud, and that is currently straining under pressures so severe that some observers have begun to wonder whether it can survive in anything resembling its present form.

The basic mechanics of peer review are straightforward, at least in principle. A researcher submits a manuscript to a journal. The journal's editor conducts an initial screening and, if the manuscript passes this threshold, sends it to two or three independent experts — the "peers" — who evaluate the work's methodology, analysis, conclusions, and significance. The reviewers provide written feedback, typically recommending acceptance, revision, or rejection, and the editor makes a final decision based on their recommendations. The entire process is, in most cases, anonymous: the reviewers do not know the authors' identities (in double-blind review) or, more commonly, the authors do not know the reviewers' identities (in single-blind review). The process is unpaid; reviewers donate their time and expertise as a form of professional service, contributing to the collective enterprise of science.

In practice, however, every step of this process is fraught with difficulties that the textbook description obscures. The first and most fundamental problem is the recruitment of reviewers. As the volume of scientific publication has exploded — the ten largest publishers alone produced approximately 2.82 million journal articles in 2022, and the number has only grown since — the demand for peer reviewers has far outstripped the supply. A 2025 article in Nature, titled "The peer-review crisis: how to fix an overloaded system," documented that journal editors are now routinely sending up to thirty-five invitations just to secure two reviewers for a single manuscript, a ratio that reflects not only the sheer volume of submissions but also the growing reluctance of scientists to take on unpaid review work when their own careers are judged almost entirely on their publication records rather than on their service to the scientific community.

The quality of reviews, even when reviewers can be found, is enormously variable. Some reviews are meticulous, constructive, and genuinely improve the manuscript; others are perfunctory, dismissive, or uninformed. The variability is partly a consequence of the voluntary, unpaid nature of the work — reviewers who are rushed, overcommitted, or simply uninterested in the manuscript they have been assigned are unlikely to produce careful, thoughtful evaluations — and partly a consequence of the lack of training or standards for the review process itself. A 2025 analysis published in PMC noted that reviewer fatigue not only compromises the quality of feedback but can lead to the failure to identify critical flaws in a paper, allowing methodologically unsound or even fraudulent research to pass through the system and into the published literature.

The problem of bias in peer review has been documented extensively and takes multiple forms. There is confirmation bias, in which reviewers are more likely to approve manuscripts that support their own theoretical positions and more likely to reject those that challenge them. There is prestige bias, in which manuscripts from well-known researchers at elite institutions receive more favorable reviews than comparable manuscripts from less prominent authors. There is gender bias, in which female researchers receive harsher reviews and longer time-to-decision than their male counterparts. And there is geographic bias, in which researchers from low- and middle-income countries face higher rejection rates, even after controlling for the quality of their work. These biases are not the result of deliberate malice — they are, for the most part, unconscious and systemic — but their cumulative effect is to distort the scientific record in ways that privilege certain voices, methodologies, and conclusions over others.

The crisis of fraud and manipulation in peer review has reached a scale that would have been unimaginable a decade ago. The entities that enable scientific fraud at scale — organizations known as "paper mills," which produce fabricated manuscripts on demand and sell authorship positions to researchers under pressure to publish — have grown into a sophisticated industry. A 2025 study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) found that fraudulent publications are doubling every 1.5 years, a rate ten times faster than the growth of legitimate scientific literature. These paper mills have learned to game the peer review system by infiltrating journals' editorial boards, compromising the identities of legitimate reviewers, and submitting fake reviews that recommend acceptance of fraudulent manuscripts. The scale of the problem is staggering: more than ten thousand articles were retracted by Hindawi, a Wiley-owned publisher, in 2023 alone, following the discovery of industrialized peer-review manipulation.

The retraction crisis, as it has come to be known, is both a symptom and a consequence of peer review's failures. When a published paper is found to contain fabricated data, plagiarized text, or other serious integrity violations, the standard remedy is retraction — the formal withdrawal of the paper from the scientific record. But retractions are blunt instruments, applied after the damage has been done, and the process is slow, inconsistent, and often resisted by the authors and institutions involved. An analysis by Retraction Watch and the research integrity community revealed that forty-five editors implicated in a single fraudulent network edited only 1.3 percent of all articles in the affected journals but were responsible for 30.2 percent of all retracted articles — a stunning concentration of malfeasance that illustrates how a small number of compromised individuals can inflict disproportionate damage on the integrity of the scientific literature.

The emergence of artificial intelligence as both a threat to and a potential savior of peer review is one of the most consequential developments in scientific publishing. On the threat side, generative AI tools have made it easier than ever to produce plausible-looking but entirely fabricated manuscripts, complete with realistic data, coherent methodology sections, and properly formatted references. An AI tool that scans manuscript titles and abstracts has flagged more than two hundred and fifty thousand cancer studies alone that bear textual similarities to articles known to have been produced by paper mills — a number that suggests the true scale of the problem is far larger than the retraction statistics indicate. On the potential-savior side, major publishers including Springer Nature, Elsevier, and Wiley are now piloting AI systems for manuscript screening, plagiarism detection, and statistical verification, tools that could dramatically improve the efficiency and consistency of the review process if they prove reliable.

The question of whether peer reviewers should be paid is one of the most contentious issues in the current reform debate. The traditional model, in which review is an unpaid service, was tenable in an era when the volume of submissions was manageable and the professional incentives were aligned — when reviewing was seen as a privilege rather than a burden, and when the community of researchers in any given field was small enough that reciprocity was the natural norm. That era has passed. The sheer volume of submissions, the pressure to publish, and the absence of any career reward for reviewing have created a situation in which the system depends on goodwill that is rapidly depleting. A 2025 article in PMC argued forcefully for the professionalization of peer review, proposing that reviewers be compensated as "professional referees" with formal training, certification, and remuneration — a model that would increase the cost of publishing but might also increase the quality and reliability of the review process.

The concept of "open" or "transparent" peer review has gained considerable traction as an alternative to the traditional anonymous model. In transparent peer review, the reviewers' identities are disclosed to the authors (and sometimes to the public), and the review reports are published alongside the final article, allowing readers to see not just the finished product but the evaluative process that shaped it. Proponents argue that transparency reduces bias, increases accountability, and produces more constructive reviews, because reviewers who know their comments will be public are less likely to be dismissive or reckless. Critics counter that transparency may discourage candid criticism, particularly from junior researchers who fear retaliation from senior colleagues, and that the public scrutiny of reviews could make the already unattractive task of reviewing even less appealing.

The reproducibility crisis, which has been particularly acute in psychology, biomedicine, and the social sciences, is intimately connected to the limitations of peer review. If the purpose of peer review is to ensure that published research is sound, then the finding that a significant proportion of published results cannot be replicated — estimates range from thirty to seventy percent, depending on the field — represents a devastating indictment of the system's effectiveness. Peer reviewers are not, of course, expected to replicate the experiments they evaluate; they are expected to assess the methodology, the statistical analysis, and the logic of the conclusions. But the reproducibility crisis suggests that these assessments are often insufficient, that reviewers routinely approve papers with underpowered studies, inappropriate statistical methods, or selective reporting of results — flaws that are sometimes difficult to detect from a manuscript alone but that become apparent when the research is subjected to independent replication.

The geographic dimension of peer review's challenges deserves attention, because the system's failures are not distributed equally around the world. China's dominance in AI-related retractions — with five hundred and fifty-one publications retracted, far exceeding any other country — has drawn particular scrutiny and has raised questions about the institutional pressures that drive publication fraud. In China, as in several other countries, academic promotion, funding, and even housing subsidies are tied directly to publication metrics, creating incentives for fraud that overwhelm the ethical norms that peer review depends on. The problem is not unique to China — publication pressure exists everywhere — but the scale and visibility of the Chinese retraction numbers have focused international attention on the systemic conditions that produce fraud, and on the inadequacy of peer review as a defense against it.

The future of peer review will almost certainly involve a hybrid model that combines human judgment with AI-assisted screening, transparent reporting with some degree of reviewer anonymity, and professional remuneration with continued reliance on voluntary community service. The American Psychological Association's 2025 initiative to strengthen and expand access to scholarly publishing reflects this hybrid vision, emphasizing that the goal is not to replace peer review but to adapt it to the realities of twenty-first-century scientific publishing — a landscape characterized by exponential growth in submissions, increasing sophistication of fraud, declining reviewer availability, and intensifying public demand for scientific reliability.

What the current crisis reveals, more than anything, is that peer review was never designed to do what we have asked it to do. It was designed as a collegial mechanism for improving manuscripts within small, trust-based communities of specialists. It was not designed to serve as the primary defense against industrial-scale fraud, the sole guarantor of scientific reliability, or the universal standard of quality across every discipline, language, and culture on earth. The demands we have placed on the system have exceeded its capacity, and the result is a growing gap between the ideal of peer review — the careful, expert, disinterested evaluation of scientific work — and the reality, which is often rushed, inconsistent, and vulnerable to the very problems it is supposed to prevent.

Reforming peer review will require not just new tools and new incentives but a fundamental reconceptualization of its role in the scientific enterprise. Peer review is one component of a much larger system of quality control — a system that also includes replication, post-publication review, statistical auditing, and the collective scrutiny of the scientific community over time — and expecting it to bear the entire weight of scientific integrity is neither fair nor realistic. The path forward lies not in defending peer review as it is or in abolishing it in frustration, but in strengthening it where it works, supplementing it where it does not, and being honest about its limitations — the same kind of honesty that science, at its best, demands of everything else.
