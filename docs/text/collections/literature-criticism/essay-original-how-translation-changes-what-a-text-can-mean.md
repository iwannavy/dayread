# How Translation Changes What a Text Can Mean
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 2026
> Note: Collection — Literature & Criticism (difficulty 6)

Every act of translation is, at its most fundamental level, an act of interpretation — a process in which a human mind (or, increasingly, an artificial one) must make thousands of decisions about what a text means before it can begin to render that meaning in another language, and each of those decisions forecloses certain possibilities while opening others that the original author may never have imagined. This is not a failure of translation but its defining condition, the inescapable consequence of the fact that languages are not parallel systems with interchangeable parts but distinct ways of organizing reality, each shaped by centuries of history, culture, and thought. To translate is not merely to find equivalent words; it is to navigate between worlds, and in that navigation, meaning is not transferred intact but transformed, sometimes subtly, sometimes profoundly.

The theoretical foundations for understanding this transformation have deepened considerably in recent decades, moving well beyond the simplistic binary of "faithful" versus "free" translation that once dominated the field. Contemporary translation studies, as reflected in a 2025 article published in Translation Review, emphasize that translation is not a linguistic endeavor alone but a literary one — a creative act that requires the translator to engage with the aesthetic, cultural, and emotional dimensions of the source text, not just its semantic content. This perspective reframes the translator not as a neutral conduit but as an active participant in the creation of meaning, someone whose choices about diction, syntax, rhythm, and register inevitably shape the reader's experience in ways that no amount of fidelity to the original can fully control.

The problem of equivalence — the question of whether it is possible to produce a text in one language that means the same thing as a text in another — has been central to translation theory since at least the time of Saint Jerome, and the contemporary consensus is that full equivalence is, in most meaningful senses, impossible. Languages differ not only in their vocabularies but in their grammars, their phonological systems, their metaphorical conventions, and their cultural presuppositions, all of which contribute to meaning in ways that resist direct transfer. The Portuguese word "saudade," for instance, denotes a deep, nostalgic longing for something or someone absent, but no single English word captures its full emotional complexity, its particular blend of melancholy and sweetness, its implication that the absence is permanent and the longing is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be inhabited. To translate "saudade" as "nostalgia" or "longing" or "homesickness" is to flatten a complex emotional landscape into a single, impoverished dimension.

The semiotic approach to translation, which draws on the work of Charles Sanders Peirce and has gained renewed attention in recent scholarship, offers a useful framework for understanding why this flattening occurs. In semiotic terms, language is a system of signs, and meaning emerges not from the signs themselves but from the relationships between signs, objects, and interpreters within a specific sociocultural context. When a text is translated, it is lifted out of one semiotic system and inserted into another, and the relationships that generated meaning in the original are disrupted, reconfigured, and sometimes severed entirely. The Japanese word "komorebi," which captures the specific beauty of sunlight filtering through leaves, carries with it an entire aesthetic tradition — the Japanese sensitivity to transience, the Buddhist awareness of impermanence, the poetic convention of noticing small, fleeting beauty — that cannot be replicated by an English phrase like "dappled sunlight," however accurate that phrase might be in a narrowly descriptive sense.

Gideon Toury, one of the most influential figures in modern translation studies, proposed that translations should be understood as "facts of the target culture" rather than reproductions of the source culture — a deceptively simple idea with radical implications. If a translation belongs to the culture that receives it, then its meaning is determined not by the author's intentions or the source text's context but by the expectations, conventions, and interpretive habits of the target audience. This means that the same source text, translated into the same target language by two different translators in two different historical periods, can produce two fundamentally different works — not because one translator is more skilled than the other, but because the target culture itself has changed, and with it the meaning-making possibilities available to the translator.

The case of literary translation makes these dynamics particularly visible, because literary texts derive so much of their meaning from formal qualities — rhythm, sound, ambiguity, irony, allusion — that are among the most difficult elements to transfer between languages. Consider the challenge of translating a pun, which depends on the accidental convergence of two meanings in a single phonological form: the pun cannot survive translation intact, because the convergence that makes it work is specific to the source language. The translator must either find a different pun in the target language — which preserves the function but changes the content — or explain the original pun in a footnote — which preserves the content but destroys the function. Either choice produces a text that means something different from the original, and the translator's decision about which dimension of meaning to prioritize is itself an interpretive act that shapes the reader's understanding.

The work of Elena Ferrante, the pseudonymous Italian novelist whose Neapolitan Quartet has been translated into dozens of languages, provides a rich contemporary example of how translation transforms literary meaning. Ferrante's prose in Italian is characterized by a particular kind of emotional directness — long, breathless sentences that accumulate detail and feeling in a way that mirrors the intensity of her narrator's inner life. Ann Goldstein's English translations, which have been widely praised, necessarily make choices about how to render this intensity — choices about sentence length, about the degree of formality in the English register, about whether to preserve the Italian syntax or restructure it for English-language readability — and each of these choices alters the texture of the reading experience, the emotional resonances that the prose evokes, and ultimately what the novel can mean to an English-speaking audience.

The concept of "untranslatability" has received renewed attention in both academic and popular discourse, though the term is somewhat misleading. As recent studies from translation agencies and linguistic researchers have emphasized, so-called untranslatable words are not entirely impossible to explain; rather, they resist reduction to a single equivalent in another language and often require full phrases, explanatory glosses, or even entire paragraphs to convey their meaning. The Swedish word "lagom," meaning "not too little, not too much — just right," encapsulates a cultural philosophy of balance and moderation that is central to Swedish identity; the Spanish "sobremesa" refers to the time spent lingering at the table after a meal, talking and enjoying company, and reflects a set of social values around communality and unhurried connection; the Southern African concept of "ubuntu," meaning "I am because we are," expresses a philosophy of shared humanity that challenges the individualism embedded in most European languages. Each of these words carries within it a worldview, and to translate any of them into English is inevitably to domesticate that worldview, to assimilate it into Anglophone categories that may be fundamentally inadequate.

The politics of translation — the question of who translates whom, and from which languages into which others, and under what institutional and economic conditions — is another dimension that shapes what translated texts can mean. English, as the dominant language of global publishing, exerts a gravitational pull that distorts the field: far more texts are translated into English than out of it, and the texts that are selected for translation into English are often those that conform to Anglophone expectations about what foreign literature should look like — exotic enough to seem interesting, but not so foreign as to be alienating. This selection process, which the translation scholar Lawrence Venuti has called "domestication," means that the version of a foreign culture that English-speaking readers encounter through translation is already curated and filtered, shaped not only by the translator's choices but by the publishing industry's calculations about marketability.

The rise of machine translation has added an entirely new dimension to these questions, because AI systems translate by identifying statistical patterns in large corpora of previously translated texts, which means that they tend to reproduce the dominant conventions and biases of those corpora rather than making the kind of creative, context-sensitive decisions that human translators make. A 2026 article in the journal Perspectives on the profession of literary translators in the age of artificial intelligence cautioned that while machine translation has become remarkably fluent at the level of individual sentences, it struggles with the qualities that make literary translation meaningful — ambiguity, irony, cultural allusion, tonal nuance — because these qualities depend on a kind of understanding that is not purely linguistic but experiential, embodied, and culturally situated. The risk, as several scholars have argued, is that the increasing reliance on machine translation will produce a homogenized global literature in which the distinctive textures of individual languages and cultures are smoothed into a bland, efficient, but ultimately impoverished "translationese."

Recent scholarship from Routledge's Studies in Literary Translation series has explored how the relationship between translation and meaning is further complicated by the phenomenon of retranslation — the practice of producing new translations of works that have already been translated, sometimes multiple times. Each retranslation is shaped by the translator's individual sensibility but also by the historical moment in which it is produced: a 2025 translation of Dostoevsky, for example, will inevitably read differently from a 1950 translation, not because Russian has changed or because the translators differ in skill, but because English has changed, and the cultural context in which English-speaking readers encounter Russian literature has changed, and the conventions of literary translation have changed. Retranslation thus reveals that the meaning of a text is not fixed but fluid, not contained within the original but constantly renewed and renegotiated through each act of translation.

The translator's visibility — or, more often, invisibility — is itself a factor that shapes meaning. In most English-language publishing, the translator's name appears in small print, if it appears at all, and readers are encouraged to experience the translation as though it were the original, as though the mediating presence of the translator did not exist. This convention of invisibility has the effect of obscuring the choices that the translator has made, creating the illusion that the text has a single, stable meaning that has been faithfully reproduced. When translators are made visible — through introductions, footnotes, or the increasingly common practice of publishing the translator's name on the cover alongside the author's — the reader is reminded that what they are reading is an interpretation, not a replica, and this awareness changes the reading experience in fundamental ways, introducing a productive skepticism about the relationship between the words on the page and the words that the author originally wrote.

The educational dimension of translation has also evolved significantly. A 2025 study published in The Interpreter and Translator Trainer argued that the focus of translation education should shift from compositional accuracy — the ability to produce grammatically correct translations — to deep meaning-making and artistry, reflecting a growing recognition that the value of human translation lies not in its accuracy (which machines can increasingly match) but in its interpretive depth, its capacity for creative risk, and its sensitivity to the cultural and emotional dimensions of language that no algorithm can fully apprehend. This shift has implications not only for how translators are trained but for how readers understand translation — not as a mechanical process of word-for-word substitution but as a creative practice that is closer to writing than to decoding.

One of the most revealing tests of how translation changes meaning is the comparison of multiple translations of the same text, a practice that literary scholars call "parallel reading." When one places side by side two or three English translations of, say, the opening sentence of Marcel Proust's "In Search of Lost Time," the differences are immediately apparent — not just in word choice but in rhythm, in tone, in the very feel of the prose — and these differences produce genuinely different reading experiences, different emotional responses, and different interpretive possibilities. What parallel reading demonstrates is that translation is not a problem with a single correct solution but an open-ended interpretive challenge, one that can be approached in multiple legitimate ways, each of which reveals certain aspects of the original while inevitably concealing others.

The implications of all this for the reader are both humbling and liberating. Humbling, because it means that any reading of a translated text is necessarily partial, filtered through the translator's sensibility and the target language's capacities, and that the original — in all its richness, ambiguity, and cultural specificity — is always, to some degree, beyond reach. Liberating, because it means that translation is not a diminished form of reading but a distinctive one, offering pleasures and insights that are available only to those who are willing to engage with a text across linguistic and cultural boundaries, accepting the losses and celebrating the unexpected gains that such engagement inevitably produces.

In the end, translation does not merely change what a text can mean — it reveals that meaning itself is not a fixed property of words on a page but a dynamic process, shaped by the languages through which it passes, the cultures in which it is received, and the individual human beings who do the painstaking, creative, endlessly challenging work of carrying a text from one world into another. To read in translation is to participate in this process, to accept that the text before you is not the text the author wrote but something new — something that carries the original within it like a memory, altered by the journey, enriched and diminished in equal measure, and ultimately, irreducibly, its own.
