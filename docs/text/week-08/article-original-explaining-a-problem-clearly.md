# Explaining a Problem Clearly
> Source: Dayread Original | Date: March 2026
> Note: Week 8 — Practical Communication Skills (difficulty 3)

One of the most useful skills in any workplace or classroom is the ability to explain a problem so that someone else can understand it quickly.

Many people struggle with this — not because they do not understand the problem themselves, but because they include too many details or start in the wrong place.

A clear explanation usually begins with a short statement of what the problem is, not how it was discovered or why it matters.

For example, instead of saying "Last week I was working on the report and noticed something strange in the numbers," it is better to start with "The sales figures for March are thirty percent lower than expected."

Once the main point is stated, the next step is to describe the context — when the problem started, what was expected to happen, and what actually happened instead.

Keeping these three elements in order — the problem, the expectation, and the reality — gives the listener a clear framework to follow.

After that, it helps to mention what you have already tried, so that the other person does not suggest solutions you have already ruled out.

A sentence like "I checked the data source and confirmed the inputs are correct, so the issue seems to be in the calculation step" saves time and shows that you have done your homework.

Avoid using vague language such as "something is wrong" or "it does not work," because those phrases force the listener to ask follow-up questions before they can even begin thinking about a solution.

Instead, be as specific as possible: name the system, the step, the number, or the behavior that is not matching what you expected.

Visual aids — a screenshot, a simple diagram, or even a quick sketch on paper — can often communicate in seconds what words take minutes to explain.

Finally, end with a clear question or request: "Can you check whether the formula changed?" is far more helpful than "What do you think?"

The goal is not to sound impressive but to make it as easy as possible for the other person to help you.

Good communicators are not the ones who use the most words — they are the ones who use the fewest words to get the right idea across.
